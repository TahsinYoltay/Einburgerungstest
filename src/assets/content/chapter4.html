Migration to Britain

Early Migration

Britain has always been a nation of immigrants. In prehistoric times, people came to Britain from the European mainland. Since that time, people from many different countries have come to make Britain their home.

For a long time, most migrants to Britain came from the continent of Europe. The Celts, Romans, Anglo-Saxons, Jutes, Vikings and Normans were all peoples who came to Britain from Europe. From the 16th century onwards, an increasing number of people came to Britain from other parts of the world.

The Huguenots

The first significant wave of immigrants from outside mainland Europe came in the 1680s, when French Protestants, called Huguenots, came to Britain to escape religious persecution in France. Many were skilled craftsmen and brought new skills to British industry.

The Huguenots (1680s):
  • French Protestants fleeing religious persecution
  • Skilled craftsmen who contributed to British industry
  • First significant non-European immigrant group

Jewish Migration

From 1880 to 1910, large numbers of Jewish people came to Britain to escape persecution in the Russian Empire and other parts of Eastern Europe. Many settled in London, particularly in the East End. This was a period of great change in British society and there was sometimes tension between existing communities and new immigrants.

Irish Immigration

Ireland was separate from Britain but under British rule. During the 19th century, Ireland suffered a number of famines. The worst of these was in the 1840s, when many people died or were forced to leave Ireland. Some came to England, others went to the USA and elsewhere. Irish people continued to come to Britain to look for work.

Irish Immigration:
  • 19th century famines, especially in the 1840s
  • Many died or emigrated to Britain, USA, and elsewhere
  • Continued migration for work opportunities

The Empire and Commonwealth

The British Empire

During the Victorian period, the British Empire became the largest the world had ever seen. At its height, it covered a quarter of the world's surface and contained a quarter of the world's population. Many people were encouraged to leave Britain to help populate the new colonies. Between 1853 and 1913, as many as 13 million British citizens left Britain to settle in the dominions and other colonies.

Migration from the Empire and Commonwealth

After the Second World War, there was a huge task of rebuilding Britain. With not enough people to do the work, the British government encouraged workers from Ireland and other parts of Europe to come to the UK. In 1948, people from the West Indies were also invited to come and work.

The Windrush Generation

The first group of West Indian immigrants came on a ship called the Empire Windrush in 1948. Many West Indians served in the British armed forces or worked in Britain during the Second World War. They were therefore familiar with Britain and in some cases had family ties to the country.

Post-War Immigration Timeline:
  • 1948: Empire Windrush brings first group from West Indies
  • 1950s-1960s: Workers from Bangladesh, India, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka
  • 1960s: Many people from Caribbean, India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh
  • 1970s: Ugandan Asians expelled by Idi Amin

Asian Immigration

During the 1950s, there was still a shortage of labour in Britain. Further immigration was therefore encouraged and many people came from the Indian sub-continent (which included Pakistan, Bangladesh and Sri Lanka). During the 1960s, immigrants also came to Britain from the Caribbean, India, Pakistan and what is now Bangladesh. Immigrants were attracted by better employment opportunities and higher wages than were available in their home countries.

Ugandan Asians

In the late 1960s and early 1970s, Uganda was ruled by Idi Amin, who expelled all people of Indian origin from the country. Many of these people, who came to be known as Ugandan Asians, settled in Britain.

Reasons for Post-War Immigration:
  • Rebuilding Britain after World War II
  • Labour shortages in key industries
  • Better employment opportunities and wages
  • Political persecution (e.g., Ugandan Asians)

Modern Migration

European Union Migration

Since the 1970s, there has been less immigration to Britain from the former colonies and more from European Union (EU) countries. Rules within the EU allow the free movement of goods, services, capital and people. This means that any citizen of an EU member state has the right to work in any other member state.

Recent Immigration Patterns

In 2004, ten new countries joined the EU, including Poland, Hungary, Czech Republic and Slovakia. Immigration to the UK from these countries increased significantly. In more recent years, people have also come to the UK seeking asylum from countries such as Somalia, Afghanistan, and Syria due to persecution or conflict.

Modern Immigration Sources:
  • EU countries (especially after 2004 expansion)
  • Asylum seekers from conflict zones
  • Economic migrants from various countries
  • Students from around the world

Impact of Immigration

Immigration has had a significant impact on British culture. New communities have brought their own cultures, traditions, food and languages. This has created a rich and diverse cultural landscape. Many festivals celebrated in Britain today have their origins in other countries, including Chinese New Year, Eid, and Diwali.

At the same time, immigrants have had to adapt to British culture and many have adopted British customs while maintaining their own cultural identity. This process of integration continues to shape modern Britain.

Cultural Impact of Immigration:
  • Rich and diverse cultural landscape
  • New festivals: Chinese New Year, Eid, Diwali
  • Variety of cuisines and traditions
  • Multiple languages spoken
  • Integration while maintaining cultural identity

The Four Nations of the UK

England

England is the largest country in the UK and where most of the population lives. England consists of 84% of the UK population and its people are called the English. The capital of England is London, which is also the capital of the UK. Other major English cities include Birmingham, Liverpool, Leeds, Sheffield, Bristol, Manchester, and Newcastle.

Scotland

Scotland is in the north of Britain. Its people are called the Scots, or Scottish people. The capital city is Edinburgh and the largest city is Glasgow. Scotland has three very distinctive areas:

  • The Highlands: A very mountainous region in the north
  • The Central Belt: Where most people live, including Edinburgh and Glasgow
  • The Southern Uplands: Hills and small towns near the border with England
Scottish Symbols:
  • National flower: thistle
  • National dress: kilt (worn by men)
  • Musical instrument: bagpipes
  • Traditional celebration: Hogmanay (New Year)

Wales

Wales is in the west of Britain. Its people are called the Welsh. The capital city is Cardiff, and other major cities include Swansea and Newport. Wales is a mountainous country. Mount Snowdon is the highest mountain in Wales. Wales has two official languages: Welsh and English. Welsh is spoken by about 20% of the population.

Welsh Identity:
  • National flower: daffodil
  • National symbol: red dragon
  • Patron saint: St David (1 March - St David's Day)
  • Two official languages: Welsh and English

Northern Ireland

Northern Ireland is on the island of Ireland. Its people can identify themselves as British, Irish, or Northern Irish. The capital city is Belfast. About half the population of Northern Ireland consider themselves unionist or loyalist and want to remain part of the UK. The other half consider themselves nationalist or republican and would like Northern Ireland to unite with the Republic of Ireland.

Northern Ireland has strong links with Scotland. Many of its people are descendants of Scottish settlers who came over 400 years ago. They are sometimes called the Scots-Irish or Ulster Scots. The Ulster Scots language is still spoken by some people in Northern Ireland.

Northern Ireland Facts:
  • People can identify as British, Irish, or Northern Irish
  • Capital: Belfast
  • Strong historical links with Scotland
  • Ulster Scots language still spoken
  • Political divisions: unionist/loyalist vs nationalist/republican

The Union

The UK is made up of England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. The full name is the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland. 'Great Britain' refers to England, Scotland and Wales. Everyone in the UK has British nationality and is called a British citizen, but people also have separate national identities and can be English, Scottish, Welsh, or Northern Irish.

The UK has been a union of different countries for over 300 years. The union with Scotland happened in 1707, creating the Kingdom of Great Britain. The union with Ireland happened in 1801, creating the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland. Most of Ireland became independent in 1922, leaving the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland.
